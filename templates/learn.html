Learn About Exoplanets

Discover the fascinating world of planets beyond our solar system and the cutting-edge science behind their detection

What are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. Since the first confirmed discovery in 1992, astronomers have found thousands of exoplanets using various detection methods. These distant worlds range from rocky planets smaller than Earth to gas giants larger than Jupiter.

Key Facts

  • Over 5,000 confirmed exoplanets discovered
  • Located in over 3,800 planetary systems
  • Multiple detection methods used
  • Kepler, TESS, and other space missions

Types of Exoplanets

Terrestrial Planets

Earth-like

Similar size and composition to Earth, potentially habitable

Super Earths

1-10 times Earth's mass, may have thick atmospheres

Mini Neptunes

Gaseous but smaller than Neptune

Gas Giants

Jupiter-like

Similar to our Jupiter, gas giant planets

Hot Jupiters

Gas giants very close to their stars

Ice Giants

Like Uranus and Neptune, far from their stars

Detection Methods

Transit Method

Detects planets when they pass in front of their star, causing a dip in brightness.

Most common method
Provides planet size
Requires edge-on orbit

Radial Velocity

Measures star's wobble caused by orbiting planet's gravity.

Provides planet mass
Works for any orbit
More massive planets easier

Direct Imaging

Takes pictures of planets directly using advanced telescopes.

Can study atmosphere
Visual confirmation
Very difficult technique

Microlensing

Uses gravitational lensing to detect planets around distant stars.

Detects distant planets
Works for any orbit
One-time events

Key Parameters for Detection

Orbital Period

Time for one complete orbit around the star

Planetary Radius

Size of the planet compared to Earth

Planetary Mass

Mass of the planet compared to Earth

Stellar Temperature

Surface temperature of the host star

Transit Depth

How much the star dims when planet passes

Transit Duration

How long the transit lasts

Impact Parameter

How close planet passes to star's center

Equilibrium Temperature

Planet's surface temperature from radiation

Insolation

Amount of stellar radiation received
